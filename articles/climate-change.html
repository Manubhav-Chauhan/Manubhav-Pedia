Climate Change

Manubhav-pedia, the free encyclopedia

Climate change refers to long-term shifts in global or regional climate patterns. Since the mid-20th century, scientists have observed that the primary cause of climate change is human activities, particularly the burning of fossil fuels, which increases heat-trapping greenhouse gas levels in Earth's atmosphere.

Overview

Climate change includes both global warming driven by human emissions of greenhouse gases, and the resulting large-scale shifts in weather patterns. Though there have been previous periods of climatic change, since the mid-20th century, humans have had an unprecedented impact on Earth's climate system.

Causes

Greenhouse Gases

Gas Sources Contribution to Warming
Carbon Dioxide (CO₂) Fossil fuel burning, deforestation 76%
Methane (CH₄) Agriculture, landfills, natural gas 16%
Nitrous Oxide (N₂O) Agriculture, fossil fuels 6%
Fluorinated Gases Industrial processes, refrigeration 2%

Human Activities

  • Burning fossil fuels for electricity, heat, and transportation
  • Manufacturing and industrial processes
  • Agriculture and livestock farming
  • Deforestation and land use changes

Effects

Environmental Impacts

  1. Temperature Rise: Global average temperatures have increased by approximately 1.1°C since pre-industrial times
  2. Sea Level Rise: Thermal expansion of seawater and melting ice sheets contribute to rising sea levels
  3. Extreme Weather: More frequent and intense heatwaves, droughts, storms, and flooding
  4. Arctic Ice Loss: Rapid melting of Arctic sea ice and glaciers
  5. Ocean Acidification: Increased CO₂ absorption makes oceans more acidic

Impacts on Society

  • Food security and agricultural productivity
  • Water resources and availability
  • Human health and disease patterns
  • Economic costs and infrastructure damage
  • Migration and displacement of populations

Scientific Evidence

The scientific consensus on climate change is that it is occurring and primarily caused by human activities. This consensus is supported by multiple lines of evidence:

  • Temperature records from weather stations, satellites, and ocean measurements
  • Ice core data showing atmospheric composition over thousands of years
  • Observations of melting ice sheets and glaciers
  • Sea level measurements and ocean chemistry changes

Solutions and Mitigation

Reducing Emissions

  • Transition to renewable energy sources
  • Improve energy efficiency in buildings and transportation
  • Develop and deploy carbon capture technologies
  • Protect and restore forests and natural carbon sinks

Adaptation Strategies

  • Build resilient infrastructure to withstand extreme weather
  • Develop drought-resistant crops and agricultural practices
  • Implement coastal protection measures
  • Create early warning systems for climate-related hazards

International Response

Global efforts to address climate change include international agreements and policies:

  • Paris Agreement (2015) - Global framework to limit warming to well below 2°C
  • Kyoto Protocol (1997) - First international treaty with binding emission reduction targets
  • United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC)
